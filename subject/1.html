Fundamental of Thermodynamics I/I :-

1. How will you find out whether a given variable is a thermodynamic property? How are those properties classified? Give examples.
→ A variable is a thermodynamic property, if and only if,
  • it has a single value at each equlibrium state, and
  • a change in its value between any two prescribed states is independent of path.
Any single variable either satisfies both of these statements or does not satisfy both. Therefore only variables which satisfy both of these statements are thermodynamic properties.

Thermodynamic properties can be classified into two main categories: intensive properties and extensive properties.
  • Intensive Properties: These properties do not depend on the size or quantity of the system. They remain constant regardless of the system's size. Some examples of intensive properties include temperature (T), pressure (P), density (ρ), specific heat capacity (c), and chemical potential (μ).
  • Extensive Properties: These properties depend on the size or quantity of the system. They scale with the system's size. Examples of extensive properties include mass (m), volume (V), energy (U), enthalpy (H), entropy (S), and total number of moles (n).
Examples of thermodynamic properties include temperature, pressure, mass, energy, and entropy.
