Geology I - Notes


Introduction
Geology :
Geology is the science that deals with one or more aspects of the earth as a planet, grouped together as earth science.
Geo → Earth, Loges → science
IAEG Definition of Geology :
The science devoted to the investigation, study and solution of the engineering and environmental problems which may arise as a result of the interaction between geology and the works and activities of man as well as to the prediction of and the development of measures for prevention or remediation of geologic hazards.
Engineering Geology:
It is defined as that branch of applied science which deals with the application of geology for a safe, stable and economical design and construction of civil engineering projects.
Branches of Geology
  1. Physical Geology : It is the study of the processs and agents which brings about the changes on the earth.
  2. Geomorphology : The study of the origin of landforms and their modification by dynamic processes.
  3. Mineralogy : It is the study of minerals.
  4. Petrology : It is the study of the composition, origin, occurrence and types of the Rocks.
  5. Ocenography : Study of the ocean and their basin.
  6. Palentology : The study of the forms of life existing in prehistoric or geologic times as represented by the fossils of plants, animals and other organisms.
  7. Geo Physics : This branch of geology deals with the application of principle of physics to the study of earth.
  8. Geo Chemistry: It is concerned with composition of earth materials and the chemical changes that occur within the earth and on its surface.
  9. Stratigraphy : The study of rock strata, especially the distribution, deposition, and age of sedimentary rocks.
  10. Structural Geology : The study of the forces that deform the earth's rocks and the description and mapping of deformed rock bodies.
  11. Economic Geology : Economic geology is the field of science that deals with the Earth's materials that have economic value. This includes metals, non-metallic minerals, and fossil fuels. Economic geologists study the formation, distribution, and extraction of these materials.
  12. Hydrology : Hydrology is the science that studies the movement, distribution, and management of water on Earth and other planets .
  13. Petroleum Geology : Petroleum geology is the branch of geology that focuses on the origin, occurrence, movement, accumulation, and exploration of hydrocarbons, primarily oil and natural gas.
  14. Engineering Geology : Engineering geology is the application of geological knowledge to engineering practice for the purpose of ensuring that geological factors are considered throughout the life cycle of an engineering project.
  15. Mining Geology : Mining geology is an applied science that combines principles of economic geology and mining engineering for the development of a mineral resource.
  16. Historical Geology : Historical geology deals with the historical development of the earth from the study of its rocks.
Differences Branches of Geology and Their Interrelationship
  • Relationship of geology with physics :
    •
Geology and physics are like two sides of the same coin when it comes to understanding Earth. Physics provides the essential laws that govern how Earth behaves, while geology gathers the evidence from rocks, minerals, and landforms.
Think of Earth's internal structure. We can't directly see it, but by using physics principles like seismic waves and how they travel through different materials, geologists can infer what the core, mantle, and crust are made of. Physics also helps us understand the movement of Earth's tectonic plates, the immense forces involved in mountain building, and even how old rocks are by using the laws of radioactive decay. In short, geological discoveries are strengthened by the foundation of physics, and new physical concepts can be inspired by what we observe on Earth.
Objective of Geology in Civil Engineering
  1. Site selection: Civil engineers rely on geologists to assess the suitability of a location for a construction project. This involves understanding the stability of the ground, the presence of faults or other geological hazards, and the availability of construction materials. By considering these factors, geologists can help to avoid costly problems later on down the road, such as foundation failures or landslides.
  2. Design: Geological input is crucial for the design of safe and sustainable civil engineering projects. Geologists can provide recommendations on the type of foundation needed for a building, the thickness of a dam, or the stability of slopes for roads and tunnels.
  3. Construction: During construction, geologists can monitor the site for potential geological hazards, such as landslides or earthquakes. They can also advise on how to mitigate these hazards and ensure the safety of workers and the public.
  4. Material selection: The properties of the soil and rock on a site will influence the type of construction materials that can be used. Geologists can help civil engineers to select appropriate materials for foundations, embankments, and other structures.
  5. Resource evaluation: In some cases, civil engineering projects may involve the excavation of rock or soil. Geologists can help to assess the quality and quantity of these resources and ensure that they are extracted in a safe and sustainable manner.
Importance of Geology in Civil Engineering
  1. Safe Site Selection: Civil engineers rely on geologists to assess the suitability of a location for a construction project. This involves understanding the stability of the ground, the presence of faults or other geological hazards, such as sinkholes or landslides , and the availability of construction materials. By considering these factors, geologists can help to avoid costly problems later on down the road, such as foundation failures or landslides.
  2. Stronger Designs: Geological input is crucial for the design of safe and sustainable civil engineering projects. Geologists can provide recommendations on the type of foundation needed for a building, the thickness of a dam, or the stability of slopes for roads and tunnels. For instance, knowing the strength and properties of the bedrock will influence the design of the foundation for a skyscraper.
  3. Hazard Mitigation: During construction, geologists can monitor the site for potential geological hazards, such as landslides or earthquakes. They can also advise on how to mitigate these hazards and ensure the safety of workers and the public. For example, if a fault line is identified near a planned construction site, geologists can recommend ways to reinforce the structures to better withstand an earthquake.
  4. Suitable Material Selection: The properties of the soil and rock on a site will influence the type of construction materials that can be used. Geologists can help civil engineers to select appropriate materials for foundations, embankments, and other structures. Understanding the soil composition is crucial for selecting the right foundation material that can support the weight of the structure.
  5. Resource Management: In some cases, civil engineering projects may involve the excavation of rock or soil. Geologists can help to assess the quality and quantity of these resources and ensure that they are extracted in a safe and sustainable manner. This includes ensuring that enough suitable rock is available for a project and that extraction doesn't cause environmental damage.
Role of Engineering Geologist
An engineering geologist acts as a translator between the complex world underground and the crucial decisions made by civil engineers. They bridge the gap between the physical properties of Earth's materials and the safe construction of our built environment.
Imagine a civil engineer wanting to build a bridge. The engineering geologist steps in to assess the soil and rock beneath the proposed location. They analyze the stability of the ground, considering factors like potential landslides or the presence of faults. By understanding these geological features, they can advise on the most suitable foundation type for the bridge to ensure its long-term stability. Throughout the construction phase, the engineering geologist remains vigilant, monitoring for any unforeseen geological hazards that could threaten worker safety or the integrity of the structure.
Importance of Engineering Geology in Nepal
Engineering geology plays a critical role in Nepal's development due to the country's unique and challenging geographical landscape.
Here's why it's so important:
Nepal's dramatic elevation changes, from the flat plains of the Terai to the soaring peaks of the Himalayas, translate to a diverse range of geological conditions. Engineering geologists help decipher these complexities. They assess the stability of slopes for road and building construction, especially crucial in mountainous regions prone to landslides. Additionally, their expertise is vital in identifying areas susceptible to earthquakes, a significant threat in a seismically active nation like Nepal.
Furthermore, Nepal's infrastructure development is crucial for progress. Engineering geologists ensure this growth happens sustainably. They advise on the selection of suitable construction materials based on the local geology and recommend methods to minimize environmental impact during resource extraction. By incorporating these geological considerations, Nepal can build safer, more resilient infrastructure that can withstand natural hazards and contribute to long-term sustainable development.
The Earth
Structure of Earth :
  1. Crust-> The Earth's crust is the outermost layer of our planet, a relatively thin, solid shell compared to the much larger mantle and core beneath it. It's the part we interact with directly, where continents and oceans are formed, and where life exists.
    Primarily composed of silicate minerals, the crust is quite heterogeneous, with varying mineral compositions and densities. There are two main types: continental crust, thicker and less dense, primarily made up of granite; and oceanic crust, thinner and denser, mainly composed of basalt.
    The Earth's crust is divided into large, rigid plates that move slowly over the underlying mantle. This movement, known as plate tectonics, is responsible for many geological phenomena, including earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building, and the formation of continents and oceans.
    Geologists use various techniques to study the crust, such as seismology, drilling, and geophysical surveys. By understanding the Earth's crust, they can gain insights into the planet's history, current dynamics, and future evolution.
  2. Mantle-> The mantle is the middle layer of the Earth, situated between the crust above and the core below. It's the thickest layer of the planet, making up about 84% of Earth's total volume.
    The mantle is primarily composed of silicate rocks, which are rich in magnesium and iron. These rocks are under immense pressure and temperature, causing them to behave in a unique way. While the upper mantle is relatively rigid, the lower mantle is believed to be more fluid-like, allowing for the movement of tectonic plates.
    The mantle plays a crucial role in Earth's geological processes. It drives plate tectonics, which is responsible for the formation of continents, oceans, mountains, and earthquakes. Additionally, the mantle is the source of magma that rises to the surface to form volcanoes.
    Studying the mantle is essential to understanding the Earth's internal structure, its evolution, and the processes that shape our planet's surface. Geologists use various techniques, such as seismology and geophysics, to investigate the mantle's properties and dynamics.
  3. Core->
    1. Outer Core-> The outer core is the layer of the Earth that lies between the mantle and the inner core. It's a thick, liquid layer composed primarily of iron and nickel, with smaller amounts of other elements like sulfur and oxygen.
      The outer core is incredibly hot, with temperatures estimated to be between 4,000 and 5,000 degrees Celsius (7,232 to 9,032 degrees Fahrenheit). This intense heat causes the iron and nickel to melt, creating a fluid layer.
      The outer core's movement is believed to be responsible for generating the Earth's magnetic field. As the molten iron and nickel flow, they create electric currents, which in turn produce a magnetic field. This magnetic field is essential for protecting life on Earth from harmful solar radiation.
      Studying the outer core is challenging due to its inaccessibility. However, scientists can infer its properties by analyzing seismic waves and the Earth's magnetic field. These observations provide valuable insights into the planet's internal structure and dynamics.
    2. Inner Core-> The inner core is the innermost layer of the Earth, located at the planet's center. It's a solid sphere composed primarily of iron and nickel, with trace amounts of other elements.
      The inner core is extremely hot, with temperatures estimated to be around 5,400 degrees Celsius (9,752 degrees Fahrenheit). It's also under immense pressure, due to the weight of the overlying layers. These extreme conditions cause the iron and nickel to solidify, despite the high temperature.
      The inner core is believed to have formed billions of years ago as the Earth cooled down. Its rotation is slightly different from that of the rest of the planet, which is thought to generate the Earth's magnetic field.
      Studying the inner core is challenging due to its inaccessibility. However, scientists can infer its properties by analyzing seismic waves generated by earthquakes. These waves travel through the Earth's interior, providing clues about the composition and structure of the inner core.
Physical Features of the Earth Surface
Mountain
Mountains are landforms that rise significantly above the surrounding terrain. They are typically characterized by their steep slopes and high elevations. Mountains can be formed through various geological processes, including:
  • Plate tectonics: When two tectonic plates collide, the crust can be forced upwards, creating mountain ranges.
  • Volcanism: Volcanic eruptions can build up layers of lava and ash, forming volcanic mountains.
  • Erosion: Over time, wind, water, and ice can erode the land, leaving behind mountains.
Mountains play a crucial role in the Earth's climate, ecosystems, and human civilization. They influence precipitation patterns, provide habitats for diverse plant and animal species, and offer opportunities for recreation and resource extraction.
Some of the most famous mountain ranges in the world include the Himalayas, the Andes, the Rockies, and the Alps.
Plateau
Plateaus are large, flat areas of land that rise above the surrounding terrain. They are characterized by their relatively level surface and steep sides. Plateaus can be formed through various geological processes, including:
Volcanism
Erosion
Uplift
Plateaus are often found in mountainous regions, but they can also occur in other landscapes. They can have significant impacts on the environment and human activities. Plateaus can influence climate patterns, provide fertile soil for agriculture, and offer unique habitats for plant and animal species.
Some famous examples of plateaus include the Tibetan Plateau, the Colorado Plateau, and the Deccan Plateau.
Shield
A shield is a large, stable area of continental crust that has been exposed to erosion and weathering over a long period of time. Shields are typically composed of very old, igneous and metamorphic rocks, often dating back billions of years.
These ancient rocks provide valuable insights into the Earth's early history, including information about the planet's formation, the evolution of its atmosphere and oceans, and the development of life. Shields are also important for understanding the structure and dynamics of the Earth's crust.
Some well-known shields include the Canadian Shield, the Baltic Shield, and the Siberian Shield. These vast, stable areas have played a significant role in the geological history of the continents and continue to be studied by geologists today.
Plate Tectonics and Mountain Building
The Himalayas, the world's highest mountain range, are a spectacular testament to the power of plate tectonics. This geological theory explains how the Earth's outermost layer is divided into large, rigid plates that move slowly over time.
The formation of the Himalayas is a direct result of a continental collision. Millions of years ago, the Indian Plate, moving northward, collided with the Eurasian Plate. As these massive landmasses pressed against each other, the Earth's crust was forced upward, creating the towering peaks we see today. This process, known as orogeny, is responsible for the formation of many of the world's major mountain ranges.
The ongoing collision between the Indian and Eurasian plates continues to shape the Himalayas. The pressure exerted by these plates causes earthquakes and other geological activity in the region. Over time, the Himalayas will continue to rise, though at a much slower rate than in the past.
