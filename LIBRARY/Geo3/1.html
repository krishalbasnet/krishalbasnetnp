1. Geology and Civil Engineering

Introduction

Geology :

Geology is the science that deals with one or more aspects of the earth as a planet, grouped together as earth science.

Geo → Earth, Loges → science

Engineering Geology:

It is defined as that branch of applied science which deals with the application of geology for a safe, stable and economical design and construction of civil engineering projects.

Branches of Geology

  1. Physical Geology : It is the study of the processs and agents which brings about the changes on the earth.
  1. Geomorphology : The study of the origin of landforms and their modification by dynamic processes.
  1. Mineralogy : It is the study of minerals.
  1. Petrology : It is the study of the composition, origin, occurrence and types of the Rocks.
  1. Ocenography : Study of the ocean and their basin.
  1. Palentology : The study of the forms of life existing in prehistoric or geologic times as represented by the fossils of plants, animals and other organisms.
  1. Geo Physics : This branch of geology deals with the application of principle of physics to the study of earth.
  1. Geo Chemistry: It is concerned with composition of earth materials and the chemical changes that occur within the earth and on its surface.
  1. Stratigraphy : The study of rock strata, especially the distribution, deposition, and age of sedimentary rocks.
  1. Structural Geology : The study of the forces that deform the earth's rocks and the description and mapping of deformed rock bodies.
  1. Economic Geology : Economic geology is the field of science that deals with the Earth's materials that have economic value. This includes metals, non-metallic minerals, and fossil fuels. Economic geologists study the formation, distribution, and extraction of these materials.
  1. Hydrology : Hydrology is the science that studies the movement, distribution, and management of water on Earth and other planets .
  1. Petroleum Geology : Petroleum geology is the branch of geology that focuses on the origin, occurrence, movement, accumulation, and exploration of hydrocarbons, primarily oil and natural gas.
  1. Engineering Geology : Engineering geology is the application of geological knowledge to engineering practice for the purpose of ensuring that geological factors are considered throughout the life cycle of an engineering project.
  1. Mining Geology : Mining geology is an applied science that combines principles of economic geology and mining engineering for the development of a mineral resource.
  1. Historical Geology : Historical geology deals with the historical development of the earth from the study of its rocks.

Differences Branches of Geology and Their Interrelationship

  • Relationship of geology with physics :

    Geology and physics are like two sides of the same coin when it comes to understanding Earth. Physics provides the essential laws that govern how Earth behaves, while geology gathers the evidence from rocks, minerals, and landforms.

    Think of Earth's internal structure. We can't directly see it, but by using physics principles like seismic waves and how they travel through different materials, geologists can infer what the core, mantle, and crust are made of. Physics also helps us understand the movement of Earth's tectonic plates, the immense forces involved in mountain building, and even how old rocks are by using the laws of radioactive decay. In short, geological discoveries are strengthened by the foundation of physics, and new physical concepts can be inspired by what we observe on Earth.

Objective of Geology in Civil Engineering

  1. Site selection: Civil engineers rely on geologists to assess the suitability of a location for a construction project. This involves understanding the stability of the ground, the presence of faults or other geological hazards, and the availability of construction materials. By considering these factors, geologists can help to avoid costly problems later on down the road, such as foundation failures or landslides.
  1. Design: Geological input is crucial for the design of safe and sustainable civil engineering projects. Geologists can provide recommendations on the type of foundation needed for a building, the thickness of a dam, or the stability of slopes for roads and tunnels.
  1. Construction: During construction, geologists can monitor the site for potential geological hazards, such as landslides or earthquakes. They can also advise on how to mitigate these hazards and ensure the safety of workers and the public.
  1. Material selection: The properties of the soil and rock on a site will influence the type of construction materials that can be used. Geologists can help civil engineers to select appropriate materials for foundations, embankments, and other structures.
  1. Resource evaluation: In some cases, civil engineering projects may involve the excavation of rock or soil. Geologists can help to assess the quality and quantity of these resources and ensure that they are extracted in a safe and sustainable manner.

For instance, knowing the strength and properties of the bedrock will influence the design of the foundation for a skyscraper.
  1. Hazard Mitigation: During construction, geologists can monitor the site for potential geological hazards, such as landslides or earthquakes. They can also advise on how to mitigate these hazards and ensure the safety of workers and the public. For example, if a fault line is identified near a planned construction site, geologists can recommend ways to reinforce the structures to better withstand an earthquake.
  1. Suitable Material Selection: The properties of the soil and rock on a site will influence the type of construction materials that can be used. Geologists can help civil engineers to select appropriate materials for foundations, embankments, and other structures. Understanding the soil composition is crucial for selecting the right foundation material that can support the weight of the structure.
  1. Resource Management: In some cases, civil engineering projects may involve the excavation of rock or soil. Geologists can help to assess the quality and quantity of these resources and ensure that they are extracted in a safe and sustainable manner. This includes ensuring that enough suitable rock is available for a project and that extraction doesn't cause environmental damage.

Role of Engineering Geologist

An engineering geologist acts as a translator between the complex world underground and the crucial decisions made by civil engineers. They bridge the gap between the physical properties of Earth's materials and the safe construction of our built environment.

Imagine a civil engineer wanting to build a bridge. The engineering geologist steps in to assess the soil and rock beneath the proposed location. They analyze the stability of the ground, considering factors like potential landslides or the presence of faults. By understanding these geological features, they can advise on the most suitable foundation type for the bridge to ensure its long-term stability. Throughout the construction phase, the engineering geologist remains vigilant, monitoring for any unforeseen geological hazards that could threaten worker safety or the integrity of the structure.

Importance of Engineering Geology in Nepal

Engineering geology plays a critical role in Nepal's development due to the country's unique and challenging geographical landscape. Here's why it's so important:

Nepal's dramatic elevation changes, from the flat plains of the Terai to the soaring peaks of the Himalayas, translate to a diverse range of geological conditions. Engineering geologists help decipher these complexities. They assess the stability of slopes for road and building construction, especially crucial in mountainous regions prone to landslides. Additionally, their expertise is vital in identifying areas susceptible to earthquakes, a significant threat in a seismically active nation like Nepal.

Furthermore, Nepal's infrastructure development is crucial for progress. Engineering geologists ensure this growth happens sustainably. They advise on the selection of suitable construction materials based on the local geology and recommend methods to minimize environmental impact during resource extraction. By incorporating these geological considerations, Nepal can build safer, more resilient infrastructure that can withstand natural hazards and contribute to long-term sustainable development.
