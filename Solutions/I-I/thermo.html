1.Introduction to Thermodynamics
  1. Define Thermodynamics.

    2. Thermodynamics is the branch of science that deals with the concepts of heat and temperature and the inter-conversion of heat and other forms of energy

  2. Define Thermodynamics Process.

    3. A thermodynamic process refers to the changes that a system undergoes in terms of its properties, such as temperature, pressure, volume, and energy, as it transitions from one equilibrium state to another.

  3. Define Thermodynamic property.

    4. A thermodynamic property is a measurable characteristic of a system that helps define its state and behavior. These properties, such as temperature, pressure, and energy, provide essential information for describing and analyzing thermodynamic processes and interactions.

  4. List important features of thermodynamic property.

    5. Some important features of thermodynamic properties:

    • State Defining: Thermodynamic properties help define the state of a system at a particular moment, providing information about its condition.
    • Measurable: Thermodynamic properties are quantifiable and can be measured using appropriate instruments and techniques.
    • Intrinsic Characteristics: Intensive properties are inherent to the substance and remain unchanged regardless of the system's size, while extensive properties depend on the quantity of matter.
    • Thermodynamic Equilibrium: Properties are meaningful when the system is in thermodynamic equilibrium, where no macroscopic changes are occurring.
    • Conservation: In a closed system, some properties, such as energy and mass, are conserved during thermodynamic processes.
    • Equation of State: Thermodynamic properties are often related through equations of state, allowing for predictions of system behavior.

  5. Define Thermodynamic state.

    6. A thermodynamic state refers to the specific condition of a system at a particular moment, characterized by a set of thermodynamic properties that collectively describe its physical properties and behavior.

  6. Define thermodynamic system, surrounding, Boundary & Universe.

    7. A thermodynamic system is a distinct portion of the universe that is chosen for analysis. It can be a physical or conceptual region containing matter and energy under study. The system is separated from its surroundings by a boundary, which can be real or imaginary. The system's properties and behavior are of interest in thermodynamic analyses.

    he surroundings of a thermodynamic system encompass everything outside the system, with which it can exchange energy or matter.

    The boundary of a thermodynamic system is the real or imaginary interface that separates the system from its surroundings.

    In the context of thermodynamics, the universe refers to the entirety of all matter and energy in existence.

  7. Define thermodynamic equilibrium.

    8. Thermodynamic equilibrium is a state of a system in which its macroscopic properties remain constant over time and no spontaneous changes occur without the influence of external factors.

  8. Differentiate control mass and control volume with examples.

    9. Difference between control mass and control volume:

    Control Mass Control Volume
    A fixed amount of mass enclosed by a boundary, and its properties are observed as it interacts with its surroundings. A fixed region in space through which mass and energy can flow, and its properties are analyzed as mass crosses its boundaries.
    Emphasizes changes in the mass and energy content of a specific substance. Emphasizes analyzing the flow of mass and energy in a defined spatial region.
    Mass remains constant within the boundary. Mass can flow into or out of the boundary.
    Observing the temperature change in a sealed container of water as it's heated. Analyzing the airflow through a nozzle in a jet engine to determine the thrust produced.
    In terms of a control mass, the mass remains constant, and the focus is on changes in energy. In terms of a control volume, mass and energy flows are considered across the boundaries.
    A closed system where only energy crosses the boundary as heat or work. An open system with a defined inlet and outlet for mass and energy.
    Mass is a fixed quantity; conservation of energy and momentum are analyzed. Mass flow rate and heat transfer are key factors; conservation of mass, energy, and momentum are considered.
    Steam flowing through a turbine blade. Air passing through the blades of a fan.

  9. How will you find out whether a given variable is a thermodynamic property?

    10. A given quantity is a thermodynamic property if it can be expressed as an exact differential. Since, the thermodynamic property depends on the end states and not on the path followed. It is a point function and a point function is always expressed as an exact differential.


  10. How are thermodynamic properties classified?

    11. Thermodynamic properties are classified based on various characteristics that describe their behavior and how they interact with a system. They can be broadly classified into two main categories: intensive properties and extensive properties.:

    • Intensive Properties:: Intensive properties are independent of the size or quantity of the system. They are intrinsic to the substance and remain the same regardless of the system's size. Intensive properties provide information about the state of a system at a specific point and are often denoted by lowercase letters. Examples of intensive properties include: Temperature (T) Pressure (P) Density (ρ) Specific volume (v) Temperature-dependent material properties (e.g., specific heat capacity, thermal conductivity)
    • Extensive Properties:Extensive properties depend on the size or quantity of the system. They are additive and scale with the system's size. Extensive properties provide information about the total amount of a property within a system. Extensive properties are often denoted by uppercase letters. Examples of extensive properties include: Mass (m) Volume (V) Total energy (U) Total entropy (S) Total internal energy (E)

  11. Differentiate between a path function and state function (point function).

    12. Difference between a path function and state function (point function):

    Path Function State Function
    Depends on the path taken during a process. Depends only on the initial and final states of the system, regardless of the path.
    Measures the amount of work or heat exchanged along a specific process path. Measures the difference in values between the initial and final states, irrespective of how the change occurred.
    Value is not uniquely determined by the initial and final states; it depends on the process history. Value is uniquely determined by the initial and final states; it remains the same regardless of the process path.
    Work done during expansion or compression. Internal energy, enthalpy, entropy, temperature, pressure.
    Not additive; depends on the specific path taken. Additive; the property of a system is the sum of its parts.
    Path functions appear in equations describing energy conservation (First Law) and work done. State functions appear in equations describing changes in energy (First Law), heat transfer, and other thermodynamic relationships.
    Can vary when the process is reversible or irreversible. Remains the same regardless of whether the process is reversible or irreversible.
    Calculating the work done during an irreversible expansion using the path-specific integral of pressure and volume changes. Calculating the change in internal energy between two states using the difference in state function values.

  12. State and explain the equality of temperature.

    13. The principle of the equality of temperature is a fundamental concept in thermodynamics that states that when two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. In simpler terms, if two systems are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are also in thermal equilibrium with each other.
    This principle can be stated as follows:
    "If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other."
    This principle is a consequence of the zeroth law of thermodynamics, which establishes the concept of temperature and provides a basis for temperature measurement and comparison. The zeroth law essentially introduces the concept of temperature equivalence: if two systems A and B are each separately in thermal equilibrium with a third system C, then systems A and B are also in thermal equilibrium with each other.
    This principle of the equality of temperature is crucial because it allows us to define temperature scales and establish a basis for measuring temperature. It ensures that temperature can be used as a reliable indicator of a system's thermal state, and it forms the foundation for concepts like the Kelvin and Celsius temperature scales.
    The equality of temperature is also used in the construction of thermometers and temperature measurement devices, which are calibrated based on the behavior of substances that exhibit consistent temperature-quantity relationships, such as the expansion of a liquid in a thermometer bulb.
    In summary, the equality of temperature is a key principle in thermodynamics that underpins the concept of thermal equilibrium, enabling the reliable measurement and comparison of temperature across different systems.

  13. Differentiate between microscopic and macroscopic view point in thermodynamic with example.

    14. Difference between microscopic and macroscopic view point in thermodynamic:

    Microscopic Viewpoint Macroscopic Viewpoint
    Focuses on the behavior and interactions of individual particles or molecules in a system. Focuses on overall system properties and behaviors without considering individual particles.
    Deals with extremely small scales, at the level of individual particles. Deals with large scales, considering the collective behavior of a vast number of particles.
    Provides insights into molecular-level processes and interactions. Analyzes observable properties, often averaged over a large number of particles.
    Studying individual particle movements, collisions, and interactions in a gas. Analyzing the overall pressure, temperature, and volume changes in a gas.
    Requires detailed knowledge of molecular behavior, interactions, and statistical mechanics. Requires macroscopic measurements and properties, often involving averages and aggregates.
    Involves complex calculations and modeling of molecular interactions. Involves simplified and averaged descriptions that disregard molecular details.
    Applied in molecular dynamics simulations, kinetic theory, and statistical mechanics. Applied in engineering analyses, thermodynamic processes, and system-level modeling.
    Emphasizes understanding emergent properties from individual particle behaviors. Emphasizes understanding overall system behavior without delving into molecular details.
    May be computationally intensive and challenging to apply to real-world systems. Averages may not capture fine-grained information and may overlook molecular-level phenomena.

  14. State and explain zeroth law of thermodynamic. Also write its application.

    15. The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. In simpler terms, this law establishes the concept of temperature equivalence: if two systems have the same temperature as a third system, then they have the same temperature as each other.
    This law is called the "zeroth" law because it was recognized after the first and second laws of thermodynamics had already been established. The zeroth law is fundamental to the concept of temperature and provides a foundation for temperature measurement and comparison.
    Explanation:
    The zeroth law can be stated as follows:

    "If two systems A and B are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system C, then systems A and B are also in thermal equilibrium with each other."
    This law is crucial because it allows us to define temperature scales and establish a basis for measuring temperature. It implies that temperature is a property that can be used to compare the thermal states of different systems. The zeroth law also enables the construction of thermometers and temperature measurement devices, as they are calibrated based on the behavior of substances that exhibit consistent temperature-quantity relationships.
    The Zeroth Law finds applications in temperature scales (e.g., Kelvin, Celsius), thermometer construction, determining thermal contact, thermal processes analysis, and temperature control systems.

  15. Explain reversible and irreversible processes with reference nature of intermediate states. Also mention their features.

    16. Reversible Process:
    A reversible process is a theoretical idealization in which a system undergoes changes between equilibrium states such that the process can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in a property. In other words, the system can return to its initial state without leaving any trace of having undergone the process. Reversible processes are considered as a reference point for analyzing the behavior of actual, practical processes.
    Features of Reversible Processes:

    • Quasi-Static Equilibrium: Reversible processes occur in a sequence of equilibrium states, with the system remaining in quasi-static equilibrium at all times.
    • Infinitesimal Changes: Each step of a reversible process involves infinitesimal changes in properties, allowing the process to be reversed by reversing these changes.
    • No Dissipation of Energy: There is no dissipation of energy as heat in a reversible process; energy is transferred as work without any loss.
    • Time Reversibility: Reversible processes can be reversed in time, and the system retraces the same path backward.
    • Idealization: Reversible processes are theoretical constructs used as benchmarks to understand the limits of system behavior.
    Irreversible Process:
    An irreversible process is one in which the system undergoes changes that cannot be exactly reversed by an infinitesimal change in a property. The system does not return to its initial state after the process, and irreversible processes are associated with dissipative effects like friction, heat transfer through finite temperature differences, and irreversibilities in real-world systems.
    Features of Irreversible Processes:
    • Departure from Equilibrium: Irreversible processes involve deviations from equilibrium states, and the system doesn't have time to adjust to infinitesimal changes.
    • Energy Dissipation: Energy is dissipated as heat due to non-quasi-static changes and irreversibilities in the process.
    • Arrow of Time: Irreversible processes follow a unidirectional flow of time, and they can't be retraced backward in the same way as reversible processes.
    • Realistic Behavior: Most natural processes in the universe are irreversible due to the presence of friction, heat transfer, and other irreversible phenomena.
  16. Explain different types of thermodynamic system.

    17. Different types of thermodynamic system:

    • Isolated System:
      An isolated system is a system that does not exchange energy or matter with its surroundings. In other words, it is completely isolated from its environment. No heat, work, or mass crosses the boundary of an isolated system. Isolated systems are theoretical constructs used to study idealized scenarios.
    • Closed System:
      A closed system, also known as a control mass, is a system that can exchange energy with its surroundings in the form of heat and work, but it does not exchange matter. The boundary of a closed system is fixed, and only energy interactions occur across the boundary.
    • Open System:
      An open system, also known as a control volume, is a system that can exchange both energy and matter with its surroundings. Open systems have specified inlet and outlet boundaries through which mass can flow in and out, and energy interactions can occur.
  17. Define Quasi-equilibrium process.

    18. A quasi-equilibrium process, also known as a quasi-static process, is a thermodynamic process in which a system undergoes changes while remaining in approximate equilibrium at each step of the process.

  18. Differentiate between heat and work.

    19. Difference between heat and work:

    Aspect Heat Work
    Mechanism Transfer of energy due to a temperature difference between a system and its surroundings. Transfer of energy due to a force acting on a system and causing a displacement.
    Direction Spontaneously flows from higher temperature to lower temperature regions. Applied to or done by a system depending on the direction of the force and the displacement.
    Type of Transfer Energy transfer through thermal contact (molecular collisions). Energy transfer through mechanical interactions (force applied over a distance).
    Symbol Q W
    Path Dependence Generally path independent; depends on initial and final states. Path dependent; depends on the specific process or path taken.
    Form of Energy Energy transferred due to temperature differences; can change internal energy. Energy transferred due to mechanical interactions; can change kinetic or potential energy.
    Measured in Joules (J) or calories (cal) Joules (J)
    Sign Convention Positive if heat is added to the system; negative if heat is lost by the system. Positive if work is done by the system on the surroundings; negative if work is done on the system by the surroundings.
    Irreversibilities Heat transfer can lead to entropy increase (irreversible process). Work can involve mechanical friction or other dissipative processes.
  19. Similarities of heat and work.

    20. Similarities of heat and work:

    • Energy Transfer: Both heat and work are forms of energy transfer. They involve the transfer of energy from one system to another or between different parts of a system.
    • Affect on System: Both heat and work can change the thermodynamic state of a system. They can alter the internal energy of a system, affecting its temperature, pressure, and other properties.
    • Path Dependence: Both heat and work can be path-dependent. The amount of heat transferred or work done can vary depending on the specific process or path taken during the energy transfer.
    • Units: Both heat and work are measured in the same unit, joules (J), in the International System of Units (SI). In some contexts, heat can also be measured in calories (cal) or other energy units.
    • Thermodynamic Processes: Both heat and work play crucial roles in various thermodynamic processes, such as isothermal, adiabatic, and isobaric processes. These processes often involve the transfer of energy in the form of heat and work.
    • Role in First Law: Both heat and work are terms in the First Law of Thermodynamics, which states that the change in internal energy of a system is equal to the sum of the heat added to the system and the work done on the system.
    • Energy Conservation: Both heat and work are forms of energy, and their transfer is subject to the principles of energy conservation. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.
    • Conversion: Heat can be converted into work and vice versa in various processes, such as heat engines and refrigerators. These processes illustrate the interconversion of heat and work based on the principles of thermodynamics.
  20. Define atmospheric pressure, gauge pressure and absolute pressure. Also write the relation between them.

    21. Atmospheric Pressure:
    Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted per unit area by the weight of the Earth's atmosphere above a certain point.
    Gauge Pressure:
    Gauge pressure is the pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure.
    Absolute Pressure:
    Absolute pressure is the total pressure at a certain point, including both atmospheric pressure and any additional pressure applied. It is the sum of the atmospheric pressure and the gauge pressure at that location.
    The relation between these pressure concepts can be expressed as follows:
    Absolute Pressure = Atmospheric Pressure + Gauge Pressure

  21. Define vaccum gauge.

    22. A vacuum gauge is an instrument used to measure the pressure of gases in a vacuum, which is a region of space with very low pressure compared to atmospheric pressure.
